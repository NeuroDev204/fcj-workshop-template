# Summary Report: AWS Study Group Team Challenge

**Date & Time:** 09:00, June 20, 2026

### Event Objectives

- Cheer on and support the teams competing in the AWS Study Group / First Cloud AI Journey team challenge, where groups of members tested their hands-on AWS knowledge against each other
- Get a close-up look at how other teams approach an AWS problem under time pressure, as a way to pick up techniques without having to compete directly
- Strengthen ties within the community by showing up for teammates and friends on competition day, not just attending study sessions

### What the Competition Was About

Rather than a lecture-style session, this was a friendly, competitive event organized within the AWS Study Group community: several teams, each made up of members who had been going through the AWS learning journey together, faced off on a series of hands-on AWS challenges - things like diagnosing a broken architecture, correctly sizing and configuring services, and answering scenario-based questions about real-world AWS use cases under a time limit. I wasn't on a competing team myself this time; I went along specifically to support and cheer for the teammates and friends who were competing.

### What Stood Out

**Watching teams think under pressure is a different kind of learning.** Sitting on the sidelines gave me a chance to see how different teams split up work under a ticking clock - some divided tasks by AWS service area, others worked the problem together out loud. It was a useful reminder that knowing AWS services individually is one thing, but coordinating with teammates to solve a messy, time-boxed problem is a separate skill entirely.

**The atmosphere was genuinely competitive but still supportive.** Even though teams were competing against each other for the same result, there was a lot of cheering, friendly trash-talk, and people from one team helping explain a concept to someone on another team once their own round had finished. It felt less like a zero-sum exam and more like the community pushing everyone's skill level up together.

**Cheering from the sidelines is still a form of participation.** Not being on a team this round didn't mean sitting passively - between rounds I found myself discussing the same challenges the competing teams were working through, quietly testing my own understanding against theirs without the pressure of the clock.

### Key Takeaways

#### Competition Formats Reveal Gaps That Study Sessions Don't

- Time-boxed, scenario-based challenges expose weak spots (like slow troubleshooting instinct) that reading documentation alone never surfaces.
- Watching multiple teams tackle the same problem in different ways is a fast way to pick up alternative approaches without having to fail at them yourself first.

#### Supporting the Community Is Part of Growing With It

- Showing up to cheer for teammates strengthens the same community bonds that make the AWS Study Group's peer learning model work in the first place.
- A competitive event doesn't have to be experienced only as a competitor - being a good supporter is its own valuable role.

### Applying to Work

- Pay closer attention to how strong teams split and coordinate work under a deadline, and try applying that division-of-labor instinct to real project sprints.
- Use scenario-based "what's broken here" style questions as a personal practice format, instead of only reviewing documentation passively.
- Keep showing up for community events even in rounds where I'm not directly competing - the exposure and relationships still compound over time.

### Event Experience

Going to this AWS Study Group team challenge as a supporter rather than a competitor turned out to be its own valuable experience. Watching friends and teammates work through hands-on AWS problems under a countdown clock made the material feel a lot more real than it does in a study session - mistakes happened live, got corrected live, and the energy in the room made the whole thing memorable in a way a normal lecture wouldn't have been.

**Lessons learned:**
- Competition formats are a great forcing function for turning passive AWS knowledge into fast, confident recall.
- Community events built around healthy competition end up reinforcing collaboration just as much as individual skill.
- Being present to support others is a legitimate and valuable way to stay engaged with a technical community, even in the rounds you're not personally competing in.
